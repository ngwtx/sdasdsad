1. Summary

The prevalence of malicious actors in the digital world has created a dynamic that compels cybersecurity defenses to adapt constantly. Attackers often zag as soon as defenders zig, making it hard for defenders to detect threats. To combat this problem, organizations have been turning to deception technology.

Deception technology allows cybersecurity teams to craft traps and gather valuable information about attacker techniques and behaviors. This information can then be used to make decisions that effectively protect the network even in the face of changing attacker methods. Deception technology has become an essential tool for organizations looking to stay one step ahead of malicious actors.

Historically, two main methods have enabled deception tech: honeypots and sandboxes. A honeypot is a digital trap that imitates an actual device on the network, with the idea of luring malicious actors into revealing themselves. Sandboxes, on the other hand, are virtual environments that restrict the activities of malware and allow post-exploitation analysis of malicious code without endangering the organization. By using these techniques together, security teams have been able to create deceptive traps that alert them when suspicious behavior is detected, allowing them to safely detect and analyze malware threats before they cause any damage.

Today, the term “deception technology” has taken on a much broader meaning. Legacy deception tech focused only on creating infrastructure that resembled Linux or Windows servers, but this is no longer effective in the ever-evolving threat landscape. With cloud infrastructures, software-defined networks, and work-from-anywhere becoming increasingly popular, modern networks have no periphery to protect. This means that innovative methods must be used to maintain security. Such methods include mapping to security frameworks such as MITRE ATT&CK or SHIELD, low code/no code features for simple customization of deception tactics, and using bait or lures for agentless detection technology.

This GigaOm Radar report highlights key deception technology vendors and equips IT decision-makers with the information needed to select the best fit for their business and use case requirements. In the corresponding GigaOm report “Key Criteria for Evaluating Deception Technology Solutions,” we describe in more detail the key features and metrics that are used to evaluate vendors in this market.

2. Market Categories and Deployment Types

To better understand the market and vendor positioning (Table 1), we assess how well deception technology solutions are positioned to serve specific market segments and deployment models.

For this report, we recognize the following market segments:

  • Small-to-medium business (SMB): In this category, we assess solutions on their ability to meet the needs of organizations ranging from small businesses to medium-sized companies. Optimal solutions reduce the administrative load for staff and have simpler deployments and easy to use alerts and dashboards.
  • Large enterprise: Here, offerings are assessed on their ability to support large and business-critical projects. Optimal solutions in this category have a strong focus on customization features, have the ability to integrate with security information and event management systems (SIEMs) or service desks, are easy to scale, and offer SaaS or on-premises models.
  • Service provider: Offerings designed with technology service providers and managed security service providers (MSSP) in mind include provisions for simplified management of multiple data sources and repositories. Moreover, service providers have unique requirements that allow them to exercise more control over the platform than a regular consumer does, as well as operate many instances of the solution independently. Having independent instances provides the necessary data separation and access controls to deliver the solution securely for each organization the provider services.
  • Federal/SLED government: Offerings for this segment are evaluated on their ability to meet the special needs of state, local, and education (SLED) and federal government clients. Successful solutions in this space are scalable, map to the MITRE ATT&CK or SHIELD frameworks, and provide forensic evidence collection capabilities.

In addition, we recognize three deployment models for solutions in this report:

  • Projection method: The projection method doesn’t rely on an agent but instead presents deception technology inside a client’s environment via manipulation of network traffic.
  • Dedicated asset: Solutions that leverage a dedicated asset generally use a specific piece of hardware, virtual machine (VM) image, cloud image, or a full operating system. These are purpose-built assets that mimic the customer environment.
  • Vanishing agent: A vanishing agent is similar to a regular agent, and it generally must be installed on a full operating system. However, what makes it a vanishing agent is that once the binary is installed, all traces of the deception technology are removed from the endpoint, leaving behind just the desired deception effect. Note that the initial deployment of these vanishing agents poses a technical challenge, which is typically handled by an incumbent endpoint management solution.

In the Maturity/Feature Play quadrant, Thinkst Canary (a long-time player in this space) provides an intuitive experience for the operators of the platform that uses a simple yet effective deception technique. CyberTrap brings a new “bait the attacker away” approach to this domain and delivers powerful deception capabilities, although it doesn’t provide two of the six key criteria. CounterCraft rounds out the group with an OS-based approach that delivers convincing deceptions to attackers.

5. Vendor Insights

Acalvio ShadowPlex

Based in Santa Clara, California, Acalvio is supported by venture capital and notable companies such as Splunk and Honeywell. Acalvio’s approach to deception technology is notable in that it leverages advanced AI technology. Additionally, the company boasts numerous patents related to its deception platform, ShadowPlex.

The ShadowPlex architecture consists of three components: Acalvio Deception Center (ADC), Deception Management Console (DMC), and sensors. ShadowPlex is designed to deploy dynamic, intelligent, and highly scalable deceptions across an organization’s network. It integrates a portfolio of 26 issued patents to form a fundamentally different architecture with low IT impact, providing scale, automation, and authenticity with the help of AI.

The ADC is either self-hosted or provided via a SaaS model. It connects to sensors in various locations (the customer data center, public or private cloud, remote sites) and provides projections to deceive attackers. The DMC is where administrators can customize the deception, enable third-party integrations, and so forth. Because the solution is delivered as SaaS and is agentless, scaling is simplified.

Recently, ShadowPlex added an identity threat detection and response (ITDR) feature to its identity protection services. This feature takes the concept of a honeypot and applies it to digital identities, widening the range of deceptive technologies that can be used to protect against potential threats in the ever-changing identity security landscape.

Acalvio uses sensors, available as virtual or physical appliances or installable on the enterprise endpoints, to act as a conduit to the ADC to project deceptive network assets to attackers. The sensor itself is not visible to attackers, who see only what customers want to be shown—vulnerable Linux, Mac, and Windows systems running inside of the ADC.

For service providers seeking a solution to meet their special requirements, Acalvio offers true multitenancy, a key function often missing in other deception technology solutions. For customer operating environments with operational technology (OT) and internet of things (IoT) devices, ShadowPlex can use decoys to give their networks the realistic illusion of containing industrial equipment like programmable logic controllers (PLCs), other controllers, and supervisory nodes. On top of that, protocols such as MOdbus, Siemens S7, and BACnet can be imitated as well, creating a convincing atmosphere for attackers roaming an OT environment.

Securing remote users presents a challenge for any security solution. Leveraging existing secure remote connections (like VPN or ZTNA), the Acalvio solution is able to place baits and lures on the endpoint, detect identity-centric attacker behaviors through ITDR features, and detect scanning and lateral movement techniques. The richest feature set is available when end users are connected to a VPN or ZTNA; however, baits and lures and some ITDR functionality continues to function even when disconnected. Although ZTNA connections are leveraged to deploy deception technology to remote users, that is the extent of this solution’s integration with ZTNA technologies.

Acalvio brings breadth and depth to dynamic deception capabilities. Via an entirely autonomous process powered by AI, dynamic deception features are active from the first instant the deception technology is activated. Additionally, as attacker behaviors are observed through interactions with the deception technology, dynamic changes occur that deliver different and relevant deceptive pieces of information to the attacker. This method of attacker engagement is highly effective at slowing an attacker’s movements, giving security teams a precious advantage.

Strengths: ShadowPlex is a well-rounded AI-powered solution that deceives attackers with realistic vulnerability projections and relevant baits and lures. The ITDR and OT/industrial control system (ICS) capabilities and its thoughtful deployment features make the Acalvio solution easy to use at scale.

Challenges: ZTNA integration is limited to enabling the deployment of deception tech to remote workers.

CounterCraft Cyber Deception

Founded in 2015, CounterCraft is a VC-backed security vendor based in San Sebastian, Spain. CounterCraft focuses on three areas: threat deception, identification of attackers, and using the intelligence gained from these activities to provide insight and context for defenders.

The CounterCraft Cyber Deception Platform (CDP) comprises a number of components, including the Deception Director (DD), which acts as the orchestration engine for the solution’s DeepSense agents. CounterCraft deployment methods include the usual on-premises and cloud options. Additionally, it has the ability to capture threat intelligence from an unusual source, like the dark web, which can provide rich context for events that would otherwise be sparse with information.

Typical on-premises and cloud deployments require a full Windows, Linux, or ARM64 endpoint with a DeepSense agent installed on it, which then communicates with the DD and is configured to fulfill its selected deception duties. In addition, the CDP lets administrators deploy bait—breadcrumbs—to provide an additional vector for deception. Of note is the ability of the CDP to be used as an MSSP tool and shared among multiple clients.

The CDP takes a broader approach to deception than other solutions do; it recognizes there is value in the information gained by observing attackers interacting with deception technology. This value is realized in the form of contextual threat intelligence that is consumed by the DD, where it is mapped to the MITRE ATT&CK framework and turned into events. Once an event is created, it can either be deposited into a SIEM tool or service desk for additional analysis, or other security platforms in the environment can use it to kick off workflows.

The CDP’s method of deploying deception components does require full systems on which agents can be deployed, which might be more resource-intensive than other vendors in this space. This prerequisite can present a challenge to organizations that may need to scale this deployment, but it’s really no different from other vendors in this space that will need to leverage an existing enterprise-grade deployment tool or perform these tasks manually. Once the agent is deployed and registered to the DD, all management tasks can be performed from the DD.

The DD makes it relatively easy to coordinate deception campaigns and roll out changes en masse to the deception hosts. Once a campaign is deployed, the DD lets you respond to potential attacks in real time via automated or manual workflows. Moreover, the DD provides built-in functionality that includes dynamic deception capabilities (deception that changes based on attacker behavior).

Strengths: The CDP delivers enriched security events, dynamic deception that misleads attackers, and notable insights into attacker tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs). It is also able to deploy bait to capture additional insights and can be easily integrated with third parties like SIEM and security orchestration, automation, and response (SOAR) systems.

Challenges: Initial deployment to multiple deception hosts requires a tool like a system center configuration manager (SCCM) or Ansible, or else appropriate time and resources must be set aside for a manual deployment.

CyberTrap

CyberTrap, a Vienna-based company, was founded in 2015 with the aim of developing deception-based technologies as the final safeguard for organizations. The company has established a solid footing in Europe and stands out from other players in the deception technology sector by providing a tailored approach to its clients. CyberTrap’s dedication to creating reliable and effective last-line-of-defense solutions underscores its commitment to ensuring that organizations stay ahead of cyber threats.

CyberTrap designed decoy systems with real OSs, services, and software to create a system that is further enhanced by a monitoring component. The beauty of this approach lies in the fact that while the attackers are engaged with CyberTrap’s deception technologies, they are being meticulously monitored. This “distract and observe” strategy provides defenders with advanced detection capabilities and higher-quality alerts, enabling them to stay ahead of potential threats.

CyberTrap’s solution is available in various deployment options, allowing customers to select the one that best aligns with their specific needs. These options include on-premises deployment via VM images, public cloud deployment, and a SaaS offering.

CyberTrap plans to release a notable feature soon—automated analysis capabilities. The field of cybersecurity is experiencing a significant shortage of skilled professionals, making it challenging to detect and respond to security threats effectively. However, CyberTrap’s solution aims to address this issue by automating the analysis process, correlating security events, and providing rich context. This approach streamlines tier 1 security operations work, enabling a more thorough understanding of potential threats and faster resolution times. By automating this critical task, CyberTrap empowers organizations to improve their overall security posture in an efficient and effective manner.

This solution is agentless, using a “run-once” approach to distribute baits and lures to endpoints. A small binary is executed on endpoints, placing deception technology, then completely removing itself, leaving no trace behind. However, remote endpoints and workers will need to be connected to corporate VPNs for this feature to work.

Mapping TTPs to MITRE frameworks such as ATT&CK and D3FEND is a valuable approach to understanding potential threat actors and their methods. The CyberTrap solution enables users to map TTPs to these frameworks, providing organizations with an additional layer of security intelligence. Furthermore, CyberTrap’s upcoming automated analysis capabilities will enhance this process by automating the identification of TTPs used in attacks.

This solution doesn’t engage in dynamic deception, focusing instead on luring attackers away from production systems with highly desirable baits. One example uses baits that can be placed into Active Directory domains, advertising an easily compromised admin account or an over-privileged standard account.

OT is an area where deception technology has been recognized to provide a great benefit; in the current version of this solution, this can be manually installed, while an upcoming version will include it in out-of-the-box deployments.

And while CyberTrap offers a range of valuable features and capabilities, it is important to know that integrations with ZTNA and VPN solutions are not currently available, although an API is offered for specialized integrations.

Strengths: CyberTrap’s deception technologies, with its in-depth monitoring, can be deployed into situations where they are likely to produce marked improvements in detection capabilities. The Active Directory baits and lures are intuitive, and the forthcoming automated analysis feature set will be a boon for resource-strapped organizations.

Challenges: This solution doesn’t engage in dynamic deception practices, nor does it offer OT deception (although OT deception is a roadmap item).

Proofpoint Illusive

Illusive, founded in 2014 by a group with a background in Israeli government agencies, brings a distinctive approach to deception technology. Proofpoint acquired Illusive in late 2022 and, as of this writing, is working on integrating its portfolio of services into the broader Proofpoint platform.

Proofpoint’s deception solution, which has a heavy emphasis on identity-based detection and deception, offers deep integrations with Active Directory, lightweight directory access protocol (LDAP), and some privileged access management (PAM) solutions. It is delivered via two services, Spotlight and Shadow. Proofpoint Spotlight is an automated platform that specializes in the continuous detection and prevention or remediation of identity data and credentials that attackers could exploit to escalate privileges, while Proofpoint Shadow deploys deceptions that resemble the data attacker’s need to access critical assets, aiding in their lateral movements.

Proofpoint’s deception method combines the deployment of a dissolvable binary with the dynamic deployment of relevant forensics to the endpoint when malicious action has been detected through its deception sensors. The dissolvable binary runs for 200 to 300 ms and vanishes from the system but leaves behind bait and lures while actively remediating the security issues before disappearing. Automated data collection features like grabbing screenshots, network stats, parent processes, and active remote sessions are designed to reduce the time needed to investigate malicious behaviors by security personnel.

Proofpoint’s management UI provides what it calls the “attacker view” of a company’s network and systems, which shows you graphically what an attacker sees when viewing your systems. Artifacts like RDP sessions and cached credentials, as well as network connections between systems, are the data that drive visual components, stitching together disparate systems into a single likely path an attacker might take to their objective inside your network. This view provides valuable insights, and it also pinpoints areas of concern in your security controls that might otherwise be missed.

The Proofpoint solution can map vulnerabilities to MITRE ATT&CK and MITRE D3FEND, and this, together with Proofpoint’s dynamic deception capabilities, can significantly improve incident response preparedness. This feature set exemplifies the deception-enriched approach to IT activities.

Proofpoint offers only basic OT or IoT deception capabilities. Assets such as network-based video cameras or building control systems can be deployed as part of a deception campaign.

The solution is currently offered in an easily deployed package that can reside on-premises or in client-provided private and public clouds. A SaaS deployment model is not yet available but should be within the year.

Strengths: The Proofpoint identity-centric approach to deception, with its agentless methods, offers a comprehensive and effective way to combat modern identity-focused attackers. With its mappings to MITRE frameworks and dynamic deception capabilities, it is also effective at accelerating incident response activities.

Challenges: For organizations seeking strong OT or IoT capabilities, this solution may not be a good fit. Additionally, SaaS-based deployment is not yet available, though it should be in 2023.

SentinelOne Hologram

In early 2022, SentinelOne, a leading extended detection and response (XDR) solution provider, acquired Attivo (a company with the stated goal of providing superior defense against in-network threats) and rapidly integrated Attivo’s deception technology into its broader XDR platform.

The Singularity XDR platform is delivered via SaaS, maintained by SentinelOne. SentinelOne Hologram is a component of the Singularity XDR; it leverages identity-based telemetry to detect malicious actions on identities. The Hologram component can be deployed into cloud and on-premises environments.

It’s unclear from the vendor website and publicly available information whether Hologram can be purchased as a standalone deception solution. If it can’t be purchased standalone, this limits its usability for certain market segments.

Hologram lets customers present decoys for a variety of assets, including servers, endpoints, applications, and even IoT devices like a point-of-sale (PoS) system and OT infrastructure devices like power substations. A defining characteristic of Hologram is its ability to hide or cloak real assets from attackers during the discovery phase of the attack kill chain. Combining cloaking mechanisms with decoys, baits, and lures allows Hologram to capture attacker behaviors earlier.

In addition, Hologram is able to leverage Window’s DLL files, which can be registered on Windows endpoints and can create the illusion of a real Active Directory infrastructure while the legitimate Active Directory infrastructure is cloaked. This deception is achieved by monitoring for the context in which certain actions are taken on an endpoint. For example, while the attacker is interacting with the fake Active Directory infrastructure, all of their TTPs are collected, analyzed, and mapped to the MITRE ATT&CK framework.

Though it appears like a natural pairing now, until recently, very few vendors were combining XDR with deception technology as SentinelOne has with Hologram. SentinelOne’s mature endpoint agent makes deploying Hologram to remote sites and users as simple as deploying an endpoint detection and response (EDR) agent, then enabling the features the organization requires from the Singularity admin portal.

Hologram allows customers to present decoy web applications, file structures, and files, collect forensic data and map it to the MITRE ATT&CK framework, and dynamically respond to attacker probes and queries. It also enables the emulation of OT devices like industrial machinery, and the collection of TTPs should an attacker interact with this device.

The usual challenges that are present with EDR/XDR solutions and other deception technologies that leverage agents exist here. For smaller organizations, manually deploying the agents will accomplish the task, while larger organizations will need to leverage incumbent endpoint management solutions.

An area where the Singularity Hologram solution falls short is its integrations with ZTNA and VPN solutions. While some solutions are leveraging one-way or two-way integrations with such technologies, SentinelOne’s Hologram is not.

Strengths: SentinelOne’s rapid integration of the Attivo deception technology into the broader Singularity XDR platform delivers a comprehensive solution for nearly all environments. The Hologram deception capabilities pair well with the analytical features of the Singularity platform, enabling rapid responses to high-confidence security events (true positive as opposed to false positive events).

Challenges: ZTNA and VPN integrations are not available within the Singularity Hologram solution.

Thinkst Canary

Thinkst Canary, a company founded by Haroon Meer in 2010 and headquartered in South Africa, aims to bring clarity to an often-chaotic cybersecurity landscape. The company offers two solutions: the Canary (a lure) and the Canarytoken (bait). To use them, users can access a SaaS management portal or set up a portal on-premises. The Canary is administered through the portal, and Canarytoken alerts can be received there as well, which allows seamless management and monitoring of the Canary solution.

The deployment and operation of both solutions is relatively easy. Leveraging either a hardware Canary that is connected to the network or a VM-based Canary, you can create and deploy a deception campaign quickly on-premises or in the cloud.

New this year are Docker container canaries, extending the canary solution into container environments like Kubernetes, EKS, and so forth. Customization is delivered through a simple interface that allows you to select multiple “personalities” or devices that can be presented to attackers. Additional customization can be configured to allow specific file shares, services, or hardware devices to be presented.

OT and ICS equipment often runs on dedicated networks that are separated from IT networks. This creates a boundary between the user realm, full of risks unique to that domain, and the OT environment. However, this also creates a demarcation between IT security controls and OT equipment. Thinkst provides canaries that can be configured to emulate ICS/PLC systems, requiring only DNS records to function. Support for OT equipment from manufacturers like Siemens, Hirschmann, and Rockwell is included.

With the evolution of deception technology comes new opportunities for vendors to innovate and expand their offerings. Thinkst is one such vendor that has taken advantage of a recent opportunity by integrating with Tailscale, an easy-to-deploy VPN-mesh built on top of Wireguard technology. Recognizing the potential of this technology, Thinkst is launching a Canary specifically designed for the Tailscale network. This innovative solution will enable deception-from-anywhere, extending coverage to remote users, data centers, cloud services, and any other location where Tailscale connectivity is present.

Canarytokens offer a variety of deception capabilities, including agentless, distributed, and remote options. The tokens are available for free and cover a wide range of items such as AWS keys, Azure login certificates, credit cards, custom binaries, and more. While the phrase “you get what you pay for” often holds true, the value provided by Canarytokens is exceptional, despite being offered at no cost. Deployment of the Canarytokens to remote endpoints is achieved the same way as for most other solutions in this space: with manual effort or an incumbent endpoint management tool.

The ease with which the Thinkst Canary solution can be deployed and adopted is one of its key strengths. Whether you are working in an on-premises or cloud-based environment, the simplicity of this tool makes it a low-effort solution to implement. Moreover, the Canarytokens offer an intriguing opportunity to leverage deception techniques in unconventional and creative ways. Their flexibility allows for a wide range of use cases, making them highly valuable and versatile tools for any organization looking to enhance their security measures.

Strengths: Thinkst Canary’s solution is both user-friendly and versatile. The Canaries and Canarytokens offer comprehensive coverage for a wide range of use cases, including OT, while the SaaS portal provides seamless integration capabilities and reduces management overhead.

Challenges: This solution does not provide MITRE ATT&CK mappings or dynamic deception based on attacker behavior.

Zscaler Deception

SmokeScreen, a Mumbai-based company founded in 2015, was acquired by the zero-trust provider Zscaler in 2021. With the rebrand and integration into the broader Zscaler product line complete, SmokeScreen is now simply “Zscaler Deception,” a feature-rich and well-designed product that has gained recognition in the market.

During the initial integration process, the product underwent an overhaul to become cloud-native, resulting in a highly scalable SaaS solution. The integration of Zscaler Deception into the Zscaler Trust Exchange Platform enables rapid deployment and integration wherever Zscaler is employed. Zscaler Deception is deeply integrated into Zscaler’s core products ZPA and ZIA, which makes Zscaler’s platform offering complete capabilities for threat prevention, reduction of attack surface, compromised threat protection, and advanced data loss prevention. Customers, however, can opt to buy Zscaler Deception as a standalone product as well.

In the realm of deception technology, dynamic deception refers to the ability of the system to respond in real-time to an attacker’s probing or interaction attempts. Typically, low-interaction deception involves responding to simple pings or displaying a secure socket shell (SSH) login banner, while high-interaction deception may involve allowing an attacker to authenticate and execute commands on a remote system. Zscaler Deception offers a range of dynamic deception capabilities that incorporate both high and low interaction levels.

One of the crucial capabilities of a security platform is the transformation of collected data into meaningful insights. Zscaler Deception achieves this transformation by generating a risk-based score for each event, which exponentially increases as multiple events are correlated. Moreover, its effectiveness is increased due to its integration with other Zscaler products, which enables deception technology to be an integral part of the risk assessment process. This feature stands out because it creates a personalized threat intelligence feed customized to the customer’s specific environment.

When forensic data needs to be shared, it can be exported into STIX or CSV format. And Zscaler Deception provides an integration with the Zscaler sandbox for more exhaustive malware analysis. Zeek, the open source intrusion detection system (IDS), is integrated to provide network-level analysis that complements the host-level analysis. The data produced by Zeek is available in the GUI, a useful capability not typically available in other security solutions, which may require the Zeek data to be exported and viewed in another application.

The ICS and IoT deception capabilities of this solution were developed in collaboration with ICS equipment manufacturers. As a result, the deception solution mimics industrial equipment very well, even going so far as to replicate the reference architecture, making the replication almost indiscernible to an attacker.

Standard support is included with the platform, but premium support will cost extra. The agentless features require your organization to have either an enterprise-level deployment solution in place or manual deployment capabilities.

The platform’s threat intelligence sources stand out. They comprise internet-based sensors that are capable of detecting targeted attacks aimed specifically at your organization, and the Zscaler global cloud furnishes data on an impressive 280 billion transactions daily (the solution uses data from customer environments as well as all other Zscaler customer environments to create threat intelligence). Moreover, this threat intelligence is currently mapped to the MITRE ATT&CK framework and will soon be aligned with the MITRE ENGAGE active defense framework. Finally, it’s worth highlighting that customizations to the deception technology can be easily achieved through low-code and no-code adjustments made via the GUI.

Strengths: Zscaler Deception blends state-of-the-art deception technology with a comprehensive range of security services, resulting in a powerful and harmonious solution. It is a meticulously crafted offering that provides a wide array of features when combined with the broader platform, features not typically available from a single vendor.

Challenges: The Zscaler solution doesn’t offer a “dedicated asset” deployment model.

6. Analyst’s Take

When embarking on a survey of the deception technology vendor landscape, one quickly realizes that while it is possible to classify vendors and solutions into general categories, each vendor’s implementation of the technology includes its own nuances. These subtle distinctions contribute to the richness and diversity of this space, enabling organizations of all sizes to identify a solution that is tailored to their specific needs.

There are various approaches to implementing and leveraging deception technology in a modern organization. Some companies may opt for a vendor-managed solution, prioritizing simplicity and effectiveness, while others may prefer an autonomous approach that amplifies the effectiveness of their existing staff. Ultimately, the choice of approach depends on an organization’s requirements and resources.

For organizations that want to add deception capabilities to their defensive lineup, Acalvio brings a class-leading set of features to the market with an all-inclusive platform approach that covers numerous deception use cases. On the other hand, if your organization is seeking a more straightforward solution, you’ll want to explore the deception technologies offered by incumbent vendors, which include Proofpoint, SentinelOne, and Zscaler. By adding a deception SKU from one of these vendors to your organization’s tech stack, the deployment process can be streamlined and simplified.

It should be noted that while Proofpoint and Zscaler offer standalone deception solutions, SentinelOne’s offering is now bundled into its XDR solution. This probably means that any organization looking to implement a deception project with SentinelOne will need to use its XDR solution as well. Nevertheless, all three vendors offer robust and reliable solutions that can meet the needs of organizations of all sizes. Ultimately, it is important for organizations to evaluate their particular requirements before selecting a vendor or solution.

Should an organization wish to deploy a deception-specific solution, CounterCraft, CyberTrap, and Thinkst are all strong feature players. As with most feature players, their solutions are simple and intuitive and don’t require tremendous training overhead to quickly get up to speed.

For organizations that prioritize autonomy and flexibility, feature players like CounterCraft, CyberTrap, and Thinkst Canary are worth considering. These companies offer specialized deception solutions that can be customized to an organization’s particular needs and requirements. An advantage of using a feature player is the simplicity and intuitiveness of their solutions.

Feature players’ solutions are typically designed with ease of use in mind. This means that organizations can quickly and efficiently deploy them without extensive training or technical expertise. That being said, it is important for organizations to carefully evaluate the capabilities of any potential deception solution provider before making a decision. While feature players may offer more autonomy and flexibility than established vendors, they may have limitations in terms of capabilities and support.
